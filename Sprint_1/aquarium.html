Albino Corydoras(Corydoras aeneus):

The Albino Cory Catfish are a captivating and popular choice for aquarium enthusiasts. They are bottom-dwellers and spend most of their time scavenging the substrate for food. These fish are not aggressive and coexist harmoniously with other non-aggressive tankmates. They’re also expert bottom feeders and natural scavenger fish. As part of the Corydoras family, Albino Cory Catfish are considered a schooling fish, which means they thrive in the company of their own kind. They can reach an average size of about 2.5 to 3 inches (6.35 to 7.62 cm) in length when fully grown.

Rainbow Platy (Xiphophorus maculatus):

Originating from Central America and Mexico, Platy Fish, also known as either “Xiphophorus maculatus” can be a great addition to your aquarium. They are usually expected to live longer in the wild than the expected two to three years in captivity. Platies vary in size based on a multitude of factors, including their sex, environment, and species, but they generally range from around two inches to three inches. Usually, the females are slightly bigger at a maximum of around three inches, while males are slightly shorter at around two inches.

Goldeneye Cichlid(Nannacara anaomola):

Scientifically known as Nannacara anomala, the Goldeneye Cichlid is a freshwater species native to South America. This cichlid's diet mainly consists of crustaceans, insects, and other small animals. Recognizing this colorful dwarf cichlid is a breeze as it possesses distinctive features. Males measure up to 2.2 inches (5.6 cm) and display a green tinge under acidic conditions. They often show a metallic blue on the head and flanks, with elongated unpaired fins. Females are smaller, turning black with a faint line running from their face to mid-tail, and bright yellow on the stomach during breeding periods.

Neon Tetra(Paracheirodon innesi):

The Neon Tetras originate from the Rio Putumayo catchment area in eastern Peru. There they live in stagnant tributaries in densely forested areas, with brown, tannin-rich water and dense riparian vegetation. In the wild, neon tetras feed primarily on small insects, insect larvae and plankton. They are swimming, peaceful schooling fish and well suited for a community tank with other, not too large and peaceful fish. At least 5, but preferably much more Neon Tetras should be kept together. The sexes are difficult to distinguish. The females are somewhat stronger and larger.
